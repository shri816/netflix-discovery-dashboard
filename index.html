The Other 96%

Netflix's recommendation engine has one job: make a library of over 16,000 titles feel infinite to viewers. Its own disclosures suggest it isn't quite managing that. About 700 titles, roughly 4% of the catalog, account for half of everything watched. What's happening to the other 96% is the more interesting problem, one the data can describe but not fully explain.

What this means

Worth naming what this data can't show: personalization systems are typically tuned and tested against popular titles, the ones with enough signal to optimize on. What actually happens to a title with a thousand views instead of a million is rarely disclosed anywhere, including here. This is a Netflix number, not necessarily a Netflix-only pattern, no other major platform shows its work at this resolution.

For the other 96% of titles, there are two very different explanations, and the numbers alone can't separate them: either these titles aren't being surfaced to the people who'd actually want them (a discovery problem), or they're being surfaced fine and still not landing (a content problem, the worse of the two). A third explanation, that overall engagement is simply declining, doesn't hold up: total hours watched has stayed within a narrow band, 90 to 96.2 billion, across all six periods, trending up if anything. Whatever is happening to the tail isn't a platform-wide slowdown.

What should worry Netflix either way is that the split isn't holding steady. The top 100 titles' share of all hours fell for about a year, then reversed hard, hitting a series high in the second half of 2025. The number of titles needed to reach 80% and 90% of all hours ticked up over the same stretch (2,628 to 2,884, and 4,446 to 4,915), which means the broad middle of the catalog, roughly ranks 200 to 3,000, held its ground or grew slightly. What's actually shrinking is the deep tail beneath that: thousands of niche titles splitting a slice of attention that keeps getting thinner.
